Leadership Perspective

A leader without a philosophy has already failed their team

Knowing what good leadership looks like is one thing. Having the courage to practice it — especially when it costs something — is another.

Set them up for success

If someone I'm guiding takes an unexpected path, I ask myself first: did I give them what they needed to succeed? Confusion is a leadership failure, not a people failure. When the brief is clear, the context is set, and the why is understood.

Composure is contagious

When the pressure is on, my job isn't to match the urgency — it's to right-size it. "Here's the one thing we need to nail, and here's what good enough looks like today." That sentence has unlocked more forward motion than any motivational push ever has.

Build the scaffold for confidence

Good coaching means meeting people where they are — not where you wish they were. The goal isn't to transfer my skills. It's to build their confidence until they don't need the scaffold anymore.

The why is the work

When I coach emerging writers, the first thing I push them toward is Sinek's question: who are you writing for, and what do they need? Set the why. People do better work when they understand the purpose they're serving.

I'd pay money for the lesson of a mistake

Every mistake someone makes is a coaching opportunity. The moment you fix it for them, you've stolen their chance to learn.

Leadership isn't a promotion. It's a practice.

The leadership challenge I'm most honest with myself about: making unpopular decisions. Not letting the desire to be liked get in the way of giving my team what they actually need. I work on this. It's the gap between knowing what good leadership looks like and having the courage to do it when it costs something.

Marketing Strategy & Infrastructure

Building the Framework That Made Expansion Marketing Possible

Situation

Valley Fiber was expanding into new communities, but there was no defined marketing structure to support it. Without a framework, campaigns risked being reactive, misaligned with construction timelines, and inconsistent across regions. Someone needed to build the system from scratch.

What I built

  • Created Valley Fiber's first structured expansion marketing framework — connecting awareness, permissions, and sales activity to construction milestones
  • Developed milestone-sequenced campaign activity across print, OOH, direct mail, and digital
  • Implemented vanity URLs to track attribution across traditionally unmeasurable channels
  • Built regionalized messaging for community-specific publications

The insight

Connecting every campaign decision to a construction phase meant the team always knew what to say, when to say it, and how to measure whether it worked. This framework is the foundation every other case in this portfolio was built on.

Email Marketing — A/B Testing

When the Offer Isn't the Problem — Friction Is

Situation

Valley Fiber wanted to win back former customers, but it wasn't clear which type of incentive would drive reconversion. The question wasn't how big the offer should be — it was what kind of offer actually changes behaviour.

What I built

I designed and executed a controlled A/B test within a winback email campaign. Group A received a $50 gift card for signing up for internet service. Group B received a $100 gift card for signing up for a bundled package. I monitored conversion rates, offer uptake, and package selection behaviour across both groups.

The insight

Not all incentives drive equal behaviour. Customers responded more strongly to offers that were simple to understand, easy to act on, and lower in commitment. Complexity works against conversion — even when offering higher-value incentives.

Crisis Communications

Thirty Minutes From Problem to Published — Keeping Customer Trust During an Outage

Situation

A phone routing issue was affecting customers, but it hadn't been flagged internally. Without swift, clear communication, customers would experience the problem with no context — eroding trust at exactly the moment it mattered most.

My approach

  • Identified the phone routing issue and recognised it as a customer communications problem, not just a technical one
  • Escalated findings with contextual intelligence to help the team understand the scope
  • Drafted, approved, and published customer communications across web and social within 30 minutes of identification
  • Maintained brand voice and customer-first framing under time pressure

The insight

Speed matters in a crisis, but clarity matters more. Customers can tolerate problems they understand. What erodes trust is silence. Getting accurate, calm communication out in 30 minutes meant customers knew what was happening before they had time to wonder.

Situation

Menopause is widely experienced but still a taboo topic in the workplace. Many employees feel embarrassed or unsupported, while employers often lack the language or confidence to address it. The challenge was to make the topic approachable, reduce stigma, and empower supportive conversations.

My approach
